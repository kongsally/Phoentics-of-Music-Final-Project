Introduction

The Super Bowl is the annual championship American football game, culminating the National Football League season on the first Sunday of February every year. Many Americans consider Super Bowl Sunday an unofficial holiday; with over 100 million viewers, it is one of the most watched televised events. Since the first Super Bowl was played in 1967, the U.S. national anthem, “The Star-Spangled Banner,” has been performed every year except one. Given the popularity of the event, many of America’s most prominent and renowned singers have performed this anthem. This notoriously difficult piece is known for its large range, and for this reason, singers are known to approach this daunting song in very distinctive ways. Our team did a phonetic comparative analysis of artists from diverse genres who have sung the anthem at the Super Bowl, including Jennifer Hudson (2009), Kelly Clarkson (2012), Renee Fleming (2014), and Idina Menzel (2015). More specifically, we analyzed both voice source and filter, such as the differential use of vibrato, vocal registers, tuning strategies, and pitch control. As leaders of genres such as R&B, pop, opera, and broadway, exploring these artists’ styles when singing the same song will allow us to better understand the characteristic qualities of a variety contemporary genres.

Tuning Strategies / Vowel Modification

From these images, we can see the vowel modification strategies that each singer employs when singing the word “free.”

Jennifer Hudson - R&B
Kelly Clarkson - Pop
Renee Fleming - Opera
Idina Menzel - Broadway

Though the microphone obscures part of the mouth, we can see that Fleming, the opera singer, is the only singer that employs typical operatic vowel modification strategies. To reach the high frequency, she drops her jaw and shortens the vocal tract and raising the larynx. This is seen in the longer “0” shape of her mouth. The FFT and LPC image indicates that Fleming’s strongest partials are in her second formant, likely because she is singing such a high note.(?) Clarkson displays similar qualities because she is singing in falsetto, but her note does not have as high a frequency, nor is she singing with a classical operatic style, so her mouth shape is not so pronounced. In contrast, the other two singers, Hudson and Menzel, have wider open mouths, in a megaphone shape. This is an indication of the vowel modification strategy they are using to raise the first formant. (Titze, Worley, and Stone 2011) analyzed the shape of the vocal tract and vowel modification strategies that singers of different styles use to reach higher notes, contrasting opera singers from belters.
We used the word “glare” to conduct a formant analysis of the vowel modification in each singer. We chose this note because it was a lower frequency in relation to the song and also has a low F1. Additionally, most of the singers would be belting (singing high notes with the chest voice) to produce this note. We saw that Fleming was the only singer whose strongest partial was under the first formant, as we would expect from an opera singer. Clarkson, Hudson, and Menzel all demonstrated strong partials under the 2nd formant, indicating that the F0 was weaker but H2 was stronger. To tune these notes, belters typically employ the megaphone mouth shaped discussed above and attempt to raise the first formant to the second one.
