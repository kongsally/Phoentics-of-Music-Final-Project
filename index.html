Introduction

The Super Bowl is the annual championship American football game, culminating the National Football League season on the first Sunday of February every year. Many Americans consider Super Bowl Sunday an unofficial holiday; with over 100 million viewers, it is one of the most watched televised events. Since the first Super Bowl was played in 1967, the U.S. national anthem, “The Star-Spangled Banner,” has been performed every year except one. Given the popularity of the event, many of America’s most prominent and renowned singers have performed this anthem. This notoriously difficult piece is known for its large range, and for this reason, singers are known to approach this daunting song in very distinctive ways. Our team did a phonetic comparative analysis of artists from diverse genres who have sung the anthem at the Super Bowl, including Jennifer Hudson (2009), Kelly Clarkson (2012), Renee Fleming (2014), and Idina Menzel (2015). More specifically, we analyzed both voice source and filter, such as the differential use of vibrato, vocal registers, tuning strategies, and pitch control. As leaders of genres such as R&B, pop, opera, and broadway, exploring these artists’ styles when singing the same song will allow us to better understand the characteristic qualities of a variety contemporary genres.

From these images, we can see the vowel modification strategies that each singer employs when singing the word “free.” Though the microphone obscures part of the mouth, we can see that Fleming, the opera singer, is the only singer that employs typical operatic vowel modification strategies. The FFT and LPC image indicates that Fleming’s strongest partials are in her first formant, showing that she is tuning the note to the formant. Since the vowel “[i]” has a first formant of around 200 Hz, which is lower than the pitch of the note that she is singing, the vowel’s first formant cannot boost the fundamental frequency or any partial (Sundberg, p. 126). Instead, she tries to adjust the first formant to bring it closer to the pitch that she is singing, by dropping her jaw and shortening the vocal tract, thus raising the larynx. This is seen in the longer “0” shape of her mouth.

Clarkson displays similar qualities because she is singing in falsetto, but her note does not have as high a frequency, nor is she singing with a classical operatic style, so her mouth shape is not so pronounced. In contrast, the other two singers, Hudson and Menzel, have wider open mouths, in a megaphone shape. This is an indication of the vowel modification strategy they are using to raise the first formant to the second harmonic, which is typically strongest while belting. Titze, Worley, and Stone 2011 analyzed the shape of the vocal tract and vowel modification strategies that singers of different styles use to reach higher notes, contrasting opera singers from belters.

We used the word “glare” to conduct a formant analysis of the vowel modification in each singer. We chose this note because it was a lower frequency in relation to the song and also has a lower first formant. Additionally, most of the singers would be belting to produce this note. We saw that Fleming was the only singer whose strongest partial was under the first formant, as we would expect from an opera singer. Clarkson, Hudson, and Menzel all demonstrated strong partials under the 2nd formant, indicating that the F0 was weaker but H2 was stronger. To tune these notes, belters typically employ the megaphone mouth shaped discussed above and attempt to raise the first formant to the second one.

Register Use

Idina Menzel

Idina Menzel sings the whole song in her chest register. Rather than switching to head voice (like the other artists) on some of the highest notes, she instead chooses to belt out in her chest register.

For example, several studies of Broadway-style musical theater singers have shown that their vocal folds make more/longer contact, resulting in less airflow and a weaker fundamental component

On “free”, the fundamental component is weaker in amplitude than 3 harmonics (H2, H4, and H5). This is characteristic of Broadway and the chest register, which is associated with high frequency harmonics of strong amplitude.

Kelly Clarkson

Kelly Clarkson similarly sings the vast majority of the song in her chest register. However, unlike Menzel, she switches to her falsetto register on some notes. For example, on “free”, she sings first begins the note in her chest register, and then switches to a pitch of 796.9 Hz in her falsetto.

The chest register is associated with a strong fundamental frequency and high amplitude harmonics, which is due to full vibration and firm contact between vocal folds.

The falsetto register is associated with less overall energy, especially in higher frequency harmonics, which is due to less vibration and less contact between vocal folds.

It should be noted that it was difficult to find a note that all four singers used vibrato on, as they come from very difficult musical backgrounds and use vibrato in differing ways. However, they all used vibrato on two notes: “glare” and “air”.
- Hudson had the lowest rate (number of cycles per second) on both “glare” and “air”.

- Fleming had the largest extent by far, followed by Hudson, and then Menzel and Clarkson on both “glare” and “air”
- All four singers were within Sundberg’s standards for both rate and extent.

Fleming uses the most vibrato, which makes sense as vibrato is an integral part of operatic song. She also shows more variation of vibrato than the other singers. This could be because opera/classical singing is characterized by freedom, emotion, release of tension, and uncontrolled vibrato, even though many believe that it is a restrictive style of singing.

Hudson uses a lot of vibrato on extended tones. Her vibrato is slower and wider than her non-Classical counterparts, and the onset of her vibrato varies throughout the piece. As an R&B/soul singer, this provides rhythm while changing timing and phrasing for expressiveness.

Menzel used the least vibrato, which was expected due to her Broadway style of song. Broadway is often characterized by belting and straight tones, with vibrato often being incorporated on the later end of notes. Moreover, vibrato allows artists to sing louder without vocal fatigue and pitch problems. Menzel is the only singer that performed the National Anthem with very minimal accompaniment, and for this reason, she did not need as much vibrato/amplification to be heard by the audience.

Clarkson’s shows very strong control over her vibrato, but her use of vibrato is mostly contained to long, extended notes. This is very characteristic of pop music; pop music utilizes effortless sounding vibrato to sustain long notes, but pop singers try to avoid overuse.

Conclusion

The Super Bowl is undeniably the pinnacle of American sports. Due to the exposure of this event, it also hosts the nation’s most iconic musicians. Their musical performances are celebrated, but often also analyzed and critiqued. Whitney Houston’s 1991 performance is widely regarded as the best National Anthem rendition in Super Bowl history. She released this performance as a single to raise money for charity, and the single went Platinum.

Many have argued that Jennifer Hudson’s rendition is of comparable quality to Houston’s rendition. Billboard ranked Hudson’s version second only to Houston’s version. Fox Sports, on the other hand, ranked Hudson’s version as the best performance in Super Bowl history, followed by Whitney Houston.

In addition, Kelly Clarkson’s version was very well-respected by viewers. Billboard ranked her as the 5th best performance in Super Bowl history.

It unfortunately seems as though Idina Menzel and Renee Fleming’s Super Bowl performances fell short of viewers’ expectations. After our analysis, we believe that this could be due to several reasons. First of all, Menzel sang the entire song a cappella, leaving very little room for pitch errors. She also sang the entire song in her chest register, which is difficult given the incredibly large range of this particular piece. In addition, Renee Fleming’s techniques and singing style are very characteristic of classical/operatic singing, and may not be best suited for a national anthem performance at the Super Bowl.
